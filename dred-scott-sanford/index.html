Road to the Civil War
Dred Scott v. Sanford
March 1857
What was it?

Dred Scott v. Sandford was a legal case where the United States Supreme Court ruled that Dred Scott, who was a slave living in a free state, was not entitled to his freedom. This case struck the Missouri Compromise as unconstitutional and portrayed the idea that Congress had no power to abolish slavery in some territories.

Why?

Dred Scott, after serving multiple masters, tried to sue for his and his wife’s freedom. This however was not successful and his case led to more conflict.

Who?

Dred Scott was an enslaved black man who attempted to sue for his freedom, which was unsuccessful. Chief Justice Roger B. Taney ruled that African Americans cannot be citizens.

How did it lead to the Civil War?

The decision made by Chief Justice Roger B. Taney outraged the abolitionists which led to many southern states seceding from the Union. This magnified the divide between anti-slavery and pro-slavery people. By striking the Missouri Compromise as unconstitutional, the case further angered abolitionists, pushing the United States closer to the Civil War.
