Road to the Civil War
Bleeding Kansas
1855-1859
What was it?

Bleeding Kansas was a period of time where there were repeated outbreaks of violence between pro-slavery and anti-slavery forces. During this time, about 55 people were killed.

Why?

John Brown was an abolitionist leader who fought in Bleeding Kansas. He was eventually executed for inciting a slave rebellion at Harpers Ferry.

Who?

Bleeding Kansas emerged from the large political debates regarding if slavery should be legal in Kansas. If Kansas was to become a slave state, it would ultimately eliminate the Missouri Compromise. Eventually, this heated debate turned into violence, which was spread over four years.

How did it lead to the Civil War?

The violence between pro-slavery and anti-slavery people was basically a mini-civil war, which established a precedent for the United States Civil War. Eventually, after the violence ceased, Kansas was voted as a free state due to antislavery settlers winning the population race. This angered pro-slavery settlers which created a larger divide between the two sides.
