Road to the Civil War
Missouri Compromise
March 1820
What was it?

The Missouri Compromise was federal legislation that prohibited slavery north of the 36° 30´ latitude line. This legislation exempted Missouri allowing it to be a slave state and admitted Maine as a free state. This was done in an effort to stop the north’s attempts to completely end slavery.

Why?

Tensions were growing between the north and the south, which prompted the U.S Congress to pass this legislation in hopes of giving both sides what they wanted. This, however, was not the case.

Who?

Henry Clay was a leading congressman who played a large role in the creation of the Missouri Compromise.

How did it lead to the Civil War?

Thirty years after its passing, the Missouri Compromise was ruled unconstitutional because both the north and south viewed it as unjust in their own ways. The south believed that the U.S Congress should not have the ability to regulate slavery. The north saw the Missouri Compromise as just a way to keep the nation in its current state and not allow it to move forward. After this ruling, many citizens rushed to vote for or against slavery, which ultimately led to the event “Bleeding Kansas”, pushing the United States closer to Civil War.
